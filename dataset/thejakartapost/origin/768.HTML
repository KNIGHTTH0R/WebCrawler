Review and Outlook

Tolerance caught between
hybridity and purity

B. Herry-Priyono, Lecturer, Postgraduate Program, Driyarkara School of Philosophy, Jakarta | Review and Outlook | Mon, January 27 2014, 2:17 PM
They need our vote: Women and children of the Shiite minority community in Sampang, Madura Island, disembark from a vehicle that took them to a ballot station in Taman district in Sidoardjo, East Java. Many members of the community who were forced to leave their homes exercised their voting rights in the East Java gubernatorial election on Aug. 29, 2013. They said they hoped the elected candidate would protect people’s interests, including their right to freedom of worship.--JP/Wahyoe Boediwardhana

In September 2013, when Home Minister Gamawan Fauzi asked Jakarta Governor Joko “Jokowi” Widodo to replace South Jakarta’s Lenteng Agung subdistrict head Susan Jasmine Zulkifli, a spate of incredulity flared up.

Some zealots protested the appointment because Susan was a Christian. It took no time for Jakarta Deputy Governor Basuki “Ahok” Tjahaja Purnama to bite back:

“Tell the home minister he should learn more about the Constitution”. It was a defense of the appointment based not on religion but on competence.

A chorus of support for Ahok and Jokowi followed.

“Minister Gamawan Fauzi, perhaps you are the one who is not the right man for the job,” was one comment.

“When immature fanatical clerics keep poisoning the minds of moderates and young followers, there will never be peace on earth,” said another.

“It’s easy. Just ‘fire’ Jokowi and move him to Merdeka Palace in 2014,” said yet another.

It was hilarious to see the home minister being put in his place. In an age short on principled politicians, we love someone who dares to speak the truth to their superior.

Although brief, it was a rare moment that pointed to what is at stake.

It unleashed the nation’s secret: a recurring tendency to deny diversity, a blot on the country’s cultural and religious life. The issue of diversity shows the difficulty of creating Indonesia in the first place.

Although racial and ethnic diversity was gradually accepted as the taken-for-granted fabric of the nation, religious differences have assumed a new level of precariousness.

This has been attested to by various surveys over the past few years. The point is plain: the growing readiness to mix with others from different racial or ethnic groups coexists with the increasing aversion to associate with others from different religious backgrounds.

Put simply, the fact that I am Malay and you’re Chinese is more palatable than the fact that I am a Muslim and you’re a Christian.

But the problem seems to run deeper, for even though we both are Muslim, the fact that I am a Sunni and you are a Shiite seems more venomous than the fact that I am Javanese and you are Ambonese.

The rift between ethnicity and religion, usually lumped together as polarizing forces, is intriguing. Why have religious differences become thorny?

Surely the difference cannot be accounted for by diversity itself, be it ethnic, racial or religious. It is explainable only by something other than existing diversity. Herein lies the importance of the menacing growth of entrepreneurs in religious fanaticism now poisoning our public life. What has contributed to this escalation?

Of course, the advent of democratization since 1998 has been greeted differently by different groups. All kinds of experiments emerged, including an open space for the marauding entrepreneurs of religious bigotry.

But there is something deeper about the present virulence of religious bigotry that fatally injures the state of tolerance. It concerns a battle between the climate of hybridity and the force of purity. How to understand the importance of this issue?

The stage on which the battle is being waged is multilayered, but one layer is crucial: the growing nature of open interactions in the present character of globalization. The exponential growth of information and communication technology that comes with it also reinforces the emergence of a new type of hotchpotch that mixes up what used to exist in isolated and segregated “purity”.

This is true not only with the free exchange of commodities but also of values, ideas and religious beliefs. There is no open interaction that doesn’t give rise to hybridity. Not only is the way we speak Indonesian littered with, say, English expressions but what we believe is also mixed up with elements from other religio-cultural traditions. Although hybridity has been with us since time immemorial, its latest incarnation is much more overwhelming.

For the custodians of religious orthodoxy, this is disconcerting. The fundamentalists are a group of purists who intend to cleanse religious doctrines of hybridity. In itself, there is nothing frightening about the agenda for doctrinal purification. The best movements of religious renewal in history also came from this act of purification, i.e., to rejuvenate doctrinal ossification by rediscovering the beginning. But there is a difference in response.

First, for those armed with open and thoughtful minds, what is taking place is less a threat than an undeniable truth of diversity, no matter how poignant.

Since diversity is made up of differences, while difference is the other side of identity, for them the task is not to deny differences but to renew religious identity by cultivating the path of dialogue; hence the importance of inter-religious dialogue.

They are constantly reminded that theological differences are there not for their own sake but to serve the common good or maslaha characterized by diversity and differences. It takes only a small step to realize that any doctrinal fixations that sacrifice the common good are not worth cultivating.

Second, but for those whom Muslim scholar Syafii Maarif calls “small-minded” custodians of orthodoxy, this climate of hybridity is poisonous and intolerable. Hybridity is seen as an aberration to be chastised by returning to older, purer doctrines. The return to the past is often taken to mean that fundamentalism is a form of traditionalism. But this is a misnomer, for their recourse to the doctrinal past is an act of confusing the past with the notion of the original – usually in the form of a single biblical text. The quest ends up in vain, for there was never any unambiguously single source of doctrine; there were only competing texts.

Since purification is taken to mean re-enacting the past, while the past remains hazy and diverse, these small-minded fundamentalists can only impose their will by hook or by crook. It is here that religious fundamentalism degenerates into bigotry. Entrepreneurs of this tribalism soon become manipulators of sectarian fears, hectoring and lashing out against anyone diverging from what they claim is purity — minorities, women, believers of other faiths or even their religious fellows who do not buy into their views. Religious radicalism is an incarnation of doctrinal fundamentalism gone awry.

Instead of a shared existence, what arises is a public mood sinking into an us-versus-them mentality. The more this goes unchecked, the more difficult it is to talk about “us” and “we”. Since a nation is made up of the possibility of a “we” based on the principle of citizenship (regardless of religious, ethnic or racial affiliation), religious tribalism becomes cancerous to public life. In turn, free speech and the democratic framework rot into a pretext for saying and committing the most barbaric things.

Tragically, the police, military, state officials or political adventurers then also use these tribal fundamentalists as proxy warriors, exacerbating the antagonistic climate of sectarian tensions.

They stay silent when bigots and religious thugs threaten or attack minorities. Even though vested with a constitutional mandate to use a stick when dealing with these violent bigots, the police become little more than examples of organized cowardice.

All this may sound like scaremongering. Yet no amount of counterclaims will prevent this predicament from being the state of affairs. As we have become familiar with, this ugly fact is usually countered by apologetic claims. Remember the protest of May 2013 against President Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono being presented the World Statesman Award for promoting religious tolerance?

By citing some laughable statistics, presidential henchmen were quick to claim Indonesia was a tolerant country: between 1977 and 2004, the number of Buddhist temples grew by 368 percent, Hindu temples by 475 percent, churches by 131 percent and mosques by 64 percent.

This is called a non-sequitur argument. Again, the fact that ordinary people wish to live in tolerance does not prevent the ground from becoming infested with nests of religious reptiles.

Will the cancer of religious tribalism cannibalize itself and eventually die out with the expansion of modern market freedom? I would contend that it won’t.

For the type of tribalism now prevailing is not a form of traditionalism, but rather a mirror-image of the modern hybridity integral to market freedom itself. It is a form of inferiority complex and insecurity toward modern/postmodern complexities involved in the hybrid character of open interactions.

That’s why its virulence goes in tandem with the modern form of market anarchism. Indeed, religious tribalism is a mirror-image of market tribalism.

The prospect is rather scary, for these bigots have become too cocky to the point that no arguments can persuade them to respect the reality of multiculturalism. That the police must be more assertive in containing their marauding exploits is stating the obvious, but this urgency has always fallen on deaf ears.

Yet, I still retain my faith in the art of government, whose leadership will be contested in the upcoming general election.

If we can elect principled leaders like Jokowi and Ahok, there is hope that this multicultural nation can enjoy a rare moment in the sun. It may be just a hope, yet it is worth cherishing even if it has changed nothing.
