IPOs of mining firms, their
impacts

Alexander Senaputra, Perth, Australia | Opinion | Mon, May 19 2014, 9:44 AM
Opinion News
  • Local elections should remain based on people’s sovereignty
  • Aceh and the rise and fall of Prabowo’s Gerindra
  • Companies see the wood for the trees

Regulatory reform seems nowhere near its end in the Indonesian mining industry. There are new or draft regulations that, although important, have received far less publicity than the raw minerals export ban.   

One such draft regulation refers to the possibility of permitting mining companies to do initial public offerings (IPOs) on the Indonesia Stock Exchange (IDX).

In other countries, mining companies often go to the stock market even before finding anything. In Indonesia, only companies making profit in the preceeding year, or starting to make profit in the next six years, can be listed on the IDX. The former is tighter because it is for listing on the IDX’s main board.   

However, it is still hard to claim that a mine will be profitable within six years unless it is proved that a certain annual production rate and sales can be maintained. In other words, mining companies have to be in the production phase for a while to do that.

The latest news mentioned that the IDX would allow pre-producing mining companies with minerals deposit data, feasibility study documents and a business plan to go for an IPO. This is done to anticipate mining companies, which are ready to advance to the production phase, going overseas for IPOs.

Mining shares always look exotic in investors’ eyes and successful capital raising through an IPO will provide mining companies some funds for project development, including for covering procurement and construction costs.

As previously mentioned, mining companies with their deposit quantified can be listed in the IDX. If the internationally accepted minerals deposit quantification method — such as the Joint Ore Reserves Committee (JORC) code — is stipulated, then independent parties with competent persons are required to estimate the “size” of mineral deposits in the examined area.  

This will avoid the likelihood of fraud as we still remember what happened with the Bre-X gold mine in East Kalimantan in 1997, where the proclamation of one of the world’s biggest gold deposits in the Busang River, Kalimantan was nothing but a lie.         

Investors are also facing other risks. Mining shares can go like a rollercoaster as explained by Frank Holmes in his article titled: “Valuation gap makes gold miners attractive but all miners aren’t created equal”.

His observation reveals that the share price of a mining company increases when the discovery of a mineral deposit is made and until the “size” of the deposit is estimated — the condition where the share price is referred to as “P”.

Normally, the increase in share price is steeper after reaching P, together with the share holder’s optimism, until unforeseen problems surface (e.g. the mining method or chemical processing is more complex than the feasibility
study predicts).

This is when the sweet turns sour and the share price tends to decrease, reaching the level referred to as “P*”. It will stay there until all issues are addresed, solved and the project is ready for the next step, by which time the share price will start climbing again.

From my observations, the position of P*, relative to P, depends on overlapping factors such as: The “size” of deposit, the commodities’ price, the actions taken by the management during that tough time, etc. This is something investors must take into account before deciding which project, when and how much to invest.     

For the government, if Law No. 4/2009 on mining is retained as the “codex” that guides all aspects in the mining business, it is important to make rules that are relevant to the IPO not in contradiction with that law and its derivatives. In reality, it is much easier said than done.

As an example, there are articles in Energy and Mineral Resources Ministerial Regulation No. 27/2013, which explains: (i) that foreign mining companies must divest part of their shares to local companies, but not through the stock market; (ii) the maximum foreign ownership allowed for different mining permits (exploration or production) and (iii) which authorities (e.g. regent, governor, ministerial regulation, Indonesian Investment Coordinating Board or law and human rights minister) must be informed when the shareholder composition is about to change.     

The above paragraph, with other regulations that cannot be mentioned one by one here, is clearly hinting how complex administration and monitoring can be in each IPO case, including the critical role of the “watchdog” appointed later.

Although it comes with a degree of speculation from the investment point of view and complexity in the administration and monitoring process, the IPO of mining companies may organically rewrite the fate of the mineral resources industry in Indonesia.


________________

The writer, who is pursuing his PhD in extractive metallurgy at Curtin University, Australia, is a freelance process engineer for several mining companies in Indonesia.
