Families of missing persons
still wait for answers

The Jakarta Post, Jakarta | Reportage | Mon, May 12 2014, 11:47 AM
Reportage News

The teenager makes jokes in an attempt to suppress her anger and keep her tears at bay as she recalls a father who used to ruffle her hair and chide her not to whine for toys; a dead mother and a bedridden grandmother whose “light is dying”.

At 18, Dinis, or Novridaniar Dinis Puspahati, fends for herself and her grandmother and has no interest in participating in street rallies after her father, Yadin Muhidin, went missing when Dinis was just 2 years old.

“I must carry on my grandmother’s mission,” to find Yadin, who was a 22-year-old Air Force cadet when he abruptly disappeared in 1998. At night, Yadin’s mother does not sleep well, sometimes crying, “Where is my son?” says Dinis.

All Dinis knows is what her mother told her when she was eight, that her father “was kidnapped by Prabowo” or Prabowo Subianto, one of this year’s presidential hopefuls representing the Gerindra Party.

Fellow retired officer Kivlan Zen, a politician from another party, blurted out on a recent television program that 13 abductees who were still missing were all dead and that he knew the location of their graves. Nine activists returned; some have become Gerindra members. Prabowo claims that he revealed everything he knew before a military ethics council, after which the military discharged him. Prabowo’s father, the late renowned economist Sumitro Djojohadikusumo, once said it made no sense that his son, then-commander of the Army's Special Forces (Kopassus), would act entirely on his own initiative. With the silence of his superiors, the families of those kidnapped can only point at one person.

“Just because of one person I have had to be the breadwinner,” Dinis blurts out. “Why me? If there was some clarity, I wouldn’t be feeling galau [teenage slang for restless, anxious]. The kidnapper did not give one thought to the impact of his actions,” she added.

On Twitter, Dinis said she frequently mentioned Gerindra leaders Prabowo and Fadli Zon. Once she tweeted, “Where’s my dad?” but added that the tweet was curiously quickly deleted. Since there have been no ransom demands nor any mention of her father, she remains in the dark. Other elderly people like her grandmother whose family members disappeared have also fallen ill, largely due to stress, relatives say.

Social media is where Dinis reaches out, seemingly oblivious to any risk, least of all of drawing the wrath of Prabowo. “I can be temperamental too, we both eat rice [...] we’re both human,” she says.

In school she was bullied because she was considered different and rebellious, being unafraid to openly criticize former president Soeharto and Prabowo. Her friends claimed Soeharto was good for, among other things, keeping gasoline prices low, but all she knew was that Soeharto and his former son-in-law, the general, were “evil” for making her fatherless, and also keeping her life at “a constant question mark”.

In filling out application forms — a chore she describes as incredibly painful — she usually leaves the space blank for her father’s name. She finds it hard to move past her personal tragedy. “Just tell me if he was tortured and is dead.”

One way of finding information about her father is by meeting activists and kidnap survivors like Mugiyanto, who cofounded the Families of Missing Persons Association (IKOHI). She only has a picture to remind her of her father, she says.

                                                                         * * *

Another missing person is Ucok Munandar Siahaan; his father says his bedroom in the family home in Depok remains unchanged, with his clothes, books and guitar, “regularly cleaned […] ready to welcome him any time”.

 “To us he lives on, as long as there is no certainty that he is dead,” says Painan Siahaan, a former employee of Bank Exim. One sign, he says, is that when he dreams of Ucok and calls out to him, his son responds. “I ask him, where have you been? And Ucok says, ‘at a friend’s.’ So there’s still hope that he’s alive, however, small,” Painan adds.

Painan says he often goes into his son’s bedroom to pray and reflect — the Batak say mangarimang-rimangi — to reflect on why his son went missing, why his wife became ill, “not regretting life, just thinking, ‘why me?’” In 1999, Painan decided to take up early retirement, partly to take care of his wife, who fell sick with depression and other complications after hunting high and low for Ucok.

 Medical costs have soared, not to mention the added expense of Painan’s past trips to spiritual figures in various areas, in the hope that he could find Ucok. Nothing materialized from these efforts, so Painan said he surrendered to God and tried legal means. With activists, families met lawmakers, and the legislature urged that the missing persons be found, and that the case should be brought to an ad-hoc human rights trial.

Kivlan’s statement, although startling, has at least brought hope of closure, says Painan.

Ucok’s name is still on the official family card; it can’t be removed. “Family cards don’t have footnotes,” says Painan, although he carries an official document from the National Commission on Human Rights (Komnas HAM) stating that his third son is missing.

“Clarity about his whereabouts, his status, is my main concern,” while law enforcers should take care of the legal side, says Painan. “That’s why I went to Komnas HAM [last week, with other families and NGOs] to state that it should immediately summon Kivlan to clarify his public statement, as the families are waiting.”

In line with custom, if their son is no longer alive, they should arrange a funeral in which particular rites must be observed. The family would first inform the church, then the civil registrar’s office of his death — while legal proceedings would determine if and why he was killed. If he is still alive, “we would have a celebration to welcome him home with gratitude,” says Painan, his choking voice turning to brief laughter.

 As a parent, his son’s unclear status “is most painful”.

 The family often gets their hopes up at night when someone rings the doorbell, he says, thinking it might be Ucok.

His mother constantly remembers the last night he was home shortly before his birthday, says Painan. He had cleaned a fish for his mother to cook, and then after dinner he said he wanted to sleep in his mom’s room, and asked her to stroke his head. “We don’t know if that was a sign.” He was going to bring friends home for his birthday. His friends called the family looking for him, but his parents thought he was in a rented house in Ciputat in Tangerang. There has been no news from him since.

Painan recalls his son telling his mother to stock up on rice, milk and noodles as the political situation was heating up. “Soeharto is about to be brought down,” Ucok told his parents. According to Painan, about two weeks after Ucok disappeared, a Kijang van followed him while he was driving the family car.

Ucok, says Painan, was attentive, always bringing his mom flowers “although they may have been from a neighbor’s garden,” he added with a laugh, his eyes glistening. Ucok was someone who friends and family turned to “for solutions”, he said.

Before the activist Munir was killed, Painan said, the family occasionally received mysterious phone calls telling them to stop looking for their son.

At 67, Painan says, “I should be sitting around at home.” But the fight keeps him going, he says. With Kivlan’s statement, there is no excuse not to do something, he adds.
