Domestic banks concerned
about shortage of talent

Khoirul Amin, The Jakarta Post, Jakarta | Headlines | Fri, May 16 2014, 8:51 AM

While domestic banks continue competing to acquire customers, it turns out that lenders are also competing for the most talented professionals, which has led to a high turnover in the banking industry.

A survey by Pricewaterhouse-Coopers (PwC) found that Indonesian banks are becoming increasingly concerned with the intense competition for the best professionals.

The study, which surveyed 82 senior bankers from the top 30 banks in the country, found that 23 percent of respondents were becoming more worried about the increased competition among banks, particularly in recruiting the industries best talents.

Titled Indonesia Banking Survey 2014, the study revealed that 44 percent of respondents considered the scarcity of quality professionals as the most pressing issue in the banking sector at present, while 37 percent of respondents were concerned about the high turnover rate, above 10 percent.

PwC found that better compensation packages (54 percent) and better promotions (37 percent) were the biggest factors pushing bankers to leave their employers for other banks, while competitiveness of remuneration packages (40 percent) and high market demand (30 percent) became the main obstacles for banks in retaining their employees.

“We have noticed this issue over the last three years, but it’s getting increasingly worrying as a number of banks had 15 to 20 percent in their turnover rate,” said PwC partner Jusuf Wibisana in a press conference on Wednesday.

Jusuf said unlike other business sectors, a high turnover rate in the banking sector was a very sensitive issue.

“With many more bankers moving from one bank to another, the possibility of consumer data leaks increases. It’s alarming,” he said, adding that a certain regulation from related institutions might be needed.

Responding to the finding, Financial Services Authority (OJK) deputy commissioner Mulya Siregar said that the OJK would possibly issue a regulation or a guiding principle regarding bankers remuneration that should be based on a bank’s core capital.

According to Mulya, bankers in managerial levels tend to make high-risk decisions, which can have a negative impact on the institution and the industry as a whole. He, however, did not provide an example.

“That is worrying,” he said, adding that wrong decisions by bankers had partly contributed to the economic downturn in Europe and the US.

Besides concern on the intense competition for talented professionals, the PwC survey highlighted possible pressure on banks’ net interest margin (NIM) as the major obstacle for the growth of the banking industry this year, with 26 percent of respondents believing this to be the case.

Jusuf said, however, that the concern was less alarming as most respondents were still upbeat that their NIM would still be around 6 percent this year, among the highest in Asia.

He said that most banks expected to see a slowdown in credit growth of around 15 to 20 percent this year from 21 percent last year.

A number of banks projected that Bank Indonesia (BI) would raise its interest rate between 8 and 9 percent this year, posing a higher credit risk, he said.

“However, with cost efficiency and more prudent credit approval processes, they believe that they can dampen the impact of a possible interest rate hike,” he said.

Meanwhile, regarding growth, PwC found that local and state-owned banks would focus more on small- and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs), which are expected to contribute 38 percent of their loan growth this year.

On the other hand, foreign and joint venture banks projected that corporate and commercial loan segments would be the biggest growth boosters, expected to contribute 39 percent of their total loans this year.
