Review and Outlook

China’s strategic developments
and their impact on relations
with ASEAN

Jusuf Wanandi, Vice Chair Board of Trustees, Centre for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS) Foundation, Jakarta | Review and Outlook | Mon, January 27 2014, 1:23 PM

The Asia-Pacific region, in particular East Asia, will be the most economically important part of the globe due to the rise of East Asian economies, with China at the center.

China understands that it is becoming a large power, and it recognizes its responsibilities as such a power, albeit it always has to balance its obligation to the common good with its domestic responsibilities, which are presenting critical challenges. It also has to learn how to use soft power, such as immediately helping to ease the misery caused by Typhoon Haiyan in the Philippines despite the existing problems between China and the Philippines.

At the same time, China is a reforming status quo power that also recognizes that it depends and thrives on the existing international system. While it wants to be more up to date with existing changes, it is only willing to do that within the established system.

China has been aiming at its peaceful rise and development, and it would like to leave behind the Cold War tenet of confrontation between the major powers or as in 19th century Europe. China would rather like to establish a community of interest. Here, the neighboring countries are important where the community of interest has to start, namely in Southeast Asia.

Two important things are: first, the building of regional economic initiatives, namely the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP) and the Trans Pacific Partnership (TPP). If the TPP is open and has relatively low ambitions, it could be useful for every country in the region to participate, while the RCEP should have higher targets than just the existing bilateral free trade arrangements between ASEAN and its many partners. Only then can a merger of the two initiatives be entertained in the future, which would be the most productive outcome for the region as a whole as opposed to one or other of the individual partnerships.

The challenge facing the RCEP is whether ASEAN’s leadership will be strong enough to forward it quickly and with a higher level of integration, while the challenge for the TPP is whether the higher level of integration (including labor, intellectual property rights, the environment, state-owned enterprises and services) can be adequately embraced by its members among the region’s emerging economies. Another hurdle is the US, and whether Congress will be willing to give President Barack Obama fast-track authority amid the current atmosphere of confrontation in the US political arena. With regard to the RCEP, China has to work together with ASEAN to make it a success because ASEAN alone may not be strong enough to promote it quickly and at a high level of integration.

Second, in the political-security field, China has proposed to set up a community of interest, primarily in its relations with Southeast Asia or ASEAN, since this is the area that China sees as being a priority. In the Southeast Asia context, the effort of establishing a regional order for peace, stability and development could be especially beneficial with regard to the South China Sea.

Also, a modus vivendi between the two great powers of China and the US, based on mutual respect, a lack of confrontation and conflict and cooperation in Asia Pacific and the world, was presented last year as a new model of relations between the two countries.

Negotiations over the South China Sea are not an effort to solve the sovereignty claims and counter-claims between four of ASEAN’s members and China (as well as Taiwan). They should be resolved bilaterally between each of the claimants. But, there is an agreement between ASEAN and China to establish confidence-building measures through implementing a Declaration of Conduct (DoC) that would govern, among other things, cooperation and joint efforts in fisheries, the environment and the exploration and exploitation of natural resources. At the same time, there is a plan to create a binding instrument in a code of conduct (CoC) for conflict prevention and management. China and ASEAN have agreed to implement the DoC and the CoC at three levels; first, between governments through the Senior Officials Meeting (SOM); second, through establishing a 1.5-track institution called the Eminent Persons and Experts Group (EPEG), and third, through the cooperation of think tanks.

With the results of the Third Plenum of the 18th Central Committee of the Communist Party of China, held on Nov. 9-14, 2012, President Xi Jinping secured the leadership that he had planned to achieve earlier, placing the 18th meeting on a par with other historic ones, like the Third Plenum of the 11th Central Committee, when Deng Xiaoping secured his power base and implemented his strategy that launched 35 years of unprecedented economic growth; or the 1993 plenum, which managed to overcome the negative impact of the Tiananmen Square massacre and saw China become a member of the World Trade Organization and project its economic trajectory to become the second-largest global economy within 20 years. President Xi is now clearly the ultimate leader. It was already clear to see when, one week before the opening of the 2012 plenum — which I attended in Beijing, Xi explained his preparation for the plenum at a meeting of former and current world leaders, the media, academics and corporate figures. He was in full command of the issues, stressing that reforms were mandatory and that they would be continued through the coming 10 years. He was calm and friendly, but full of certainty and authority.

It may be said that greater reform may be the fruit of new policies and changes to come, as some proposals and decisions had been made before and had been partially implemented. Together they can constitute truly significant reform. We can expect stability and authority to be maintained under Xi’s leadership. While he is not Deng Xiaoping, who gained full power after the 1978 plenum, Xi can become a reasonably strong powerhouse himself.

The strategic situation in East Asia, which many have said looks similar to the strategic developments in Europe at the end of the 19th century and the first quarter of the 20th century — namely that of countries enjoying close economic relations but suspicious of one another on security matters that resulted in two world wars, may not be entirely true. Now with nuclear weapons, it is unusual for countries to engage in large-scale wars; and because of the existence of various institutions and cooperation among countries in East Asia, which is quite intense and profound, it is doubtful that tensions will be allowed to escalate into all-out conflicts. Also, there is no one overarching power in the region acting against the existing international order, as was the case with Nazi Germany before World War II or the former Soviet Union during the Cold War. ASEAN’s role as an interlocutor of the major powers in East Asia can be of critical importance for the region’s future, because it is acceptable to all sides.

To be really effective, ASEAN needs to foster greater integration and more vigorous cooperation among its members. The ASEAN Economic Community (AEC) is a step in the right direction and it is planned to be achieved by the end of 2015. In addition, other major countries, especially China and the US, should support ASEAN’s effort to create a community of interest in East Asia for the future.
