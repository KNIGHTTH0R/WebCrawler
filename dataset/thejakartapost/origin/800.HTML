Review and Outlook

Guarding prudence amid
election noise

Djisman Simandjuntak, Chair, Board of Directors, Centre for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS) Foundation, Jakarta | Review and Outlook | Mon, January 27 2014, 2:28 PM

The depreciation of the rupiah against the US dollar by 6.5 percent in 2012 and 21.2 percent in 2013 was a bitter wake-up call to Indonesian leaders and, in particular, politicians, who in recent times have painted rosy pictures of Indonesia’s assured ascent from its current lower-middle income status to an upper-middle income economy by around 2025.

While such aspiration is legitimate and seemingly within Indonesia’s reach, drawing lessons from the discouraging developments in 2013 and communicating them as widely as possible is a political imperative before the noise of election campaigns deafens people in responsible positions.

To some extent, the rupiah’s fall against the US dollar is part of a global phenomenon, as the dollar has strengthened in recent months on a brightening economic outlook in the United States and the signals about stimulus tapering become increasingly clear and credible. However, a greater cause of the fall is a repetitive cycle that to this day haunts Indonesian business practitioners and households who had to swallow severe capital losses in the wake of 2013’s severe depreciation as they did in 1978, 1983, 1986 and 1997/1998.

Sudden depreciation has been such a regular feature of the Indonesian economy that it is widely believed to strike every decade or so irrespective of what politicians and officials say to the contrary. That the last episode of exchange rate stability lasted for more than 10 years is somewhat exceptional in recent history and is partly related to the very deep undervaluation that followed the crisis of 1997/1998 when the real effective exchange rate (REER) fell to 37 in June 1998 and rose only gradually in subsequent years, according to the exchange rate time series reported by the Bank for International Settlements (BIS).

The mechanics of the most recent cycle is very similar to that of past cycles. It set forth in the wake of a jump in capital inflows. Indonesia’s international equity and debt liability position rose by US$89 billion in 2009 and $111.3 billion in 2010.

The increases moderated in 2011 and 2012 but still drove up the liability position, from $228 billion in 2008 to $533 billion in 2012, implying a huge inflow indeed. Triggered by China’s turning into a “Resource Black Hole”, characterized by a very aggressive reserve acquisition around the globe, the commodity market boomed in 2011, providing a strong tailwind to Indonesia’s upward economic cycle.

After a strong rebound in 2010, nominal commodity prices in 2011 rose by 36 percent for crude petroleum, 23 percent for coal, 39 percent for coffee, 39 percent for palm oil, 32 percent for rubber, 17 percent for copper and 28 percent for tin. Indonesia’s total exports in 2011 shot up by 27 percent over the previous year, driven by primary commodities, which in 2011 accounted for 69 percent of total exports.

With the huge foreign exchange inflows from trade in goods and capital flows, a strong expansion followed in the financial services industry. Bank loans in the period of 2008-2012 rose twice as fast as bank deposits, fueling a rapid increase in asset prices and a concomitant investment in real estate. For bank loans, the four-year period was one of rapid expansion, with lending to non-business sectors such as housing and motor vehicle ownership growing at a rapid pace.

Indonesians are taught since childhood that ups and downs are the nature of a rolling wheel. The export surge in 2011 was, indeed, short-lived. Its fall was inherent in rise, as suggested by the “resource curse” hypothesis. Total exports fell from $200.8 billion in 2011 to $188.5 billion in 2012 and probably not more than $180 billion in 2013, or a fall of roughly $20 billion over the last two years.

As for imports, the strong foreign exchange inflows in the three-year period of 2009-2011, together with various domestic policies — the incomprehensibly generous and socially questionable fuel subsidy, a very pro-growth policy toward the airline industry and a failed one in urban transportation — drove up the import of goods, services and income payments overseas. Goods imports rose tremendously in 2012 compared to 2000. Oil and gas imports skyrocketed to over $43 billion, an increase of seven times. Aircraft purchases were $4.5 billion, up from less than $1 billion in 2000; motor vehicle imports rose to $9.8 billion from less than $2 billion; telecommunications equipment to $5 billion from less than $200 million; and sugar to $1.9 billion from $300 million.

At the same time, the rapid increase in foreign investment in the years after the global financial crisis of 2008 led to a huge outflow of investment income totaling $29.2 billion in 2011 and $29.4 billion in 2012, often exceeding the combined inflow of foreign direct and portfolio investments.

In the meantime, the commodity boom came to an end as economic conditions deteriorated in major developed economies and the core emerging economies of China and India. Average prices in July-September 2013 compared to 2011 fell by 36 percent for coal, 49 percent for palm oil, 46 percent for rubber, 20 percent for copper and 39 percent for nickel, while oil prices stayed basically the same. Total exports in 2012 fell by 6.1 percent.

The current-account deficit rose to an imprudent level of 4 percent of gross domestic product (GDP). Confronted with the new numbers on the one hand and the flight to quality among investors on the other, foreign investment inflows to Indonesia decelerated to an extent that is insufficient to cover the current-account deficit. International reserves shrunk from the peak of $124.6 billion on Aug. 26, 2011 to $96.7 billion on Nov. 13, 2013. The pressure on the rupiah was first very incremental but exacerbated considerably in the latter part of 2013 and continued in the first few days of 2014, signaling that it may be difficult to reverse.

Bank Indonesia (BI) did initiate a number of tightening measures, if relatively late, as one can argue in hindsight. The policy rate was raised and bank lending rates followed quickly as a widening interest rate spread. Second tier banks with their dependence on high-cost funding may have to struggle with rising non-performing loans in the course of 2014.

Consumption of durable goods, real estate investment and fixed capital investment appear to have slowed down or are likely to do so in the very near future. They combine with the improving economic outlook in the major Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) economies, and the associated soft recovery in the global commodity market, to reverse the trade deficit in the last quarter of 2013.

Deficits in services and investment income are also bound to narrow as the pace of economic growth decelerates. The current-account deficit is very likely to shrink. In other words, Indonesians can expect to see a less frightening development in 2014 than the relatively steep depreciation of the rupiah in the last four months of 2013 tends to suggest, assuming that no severe external shock erupts suddenly.

Obviously, economic change is anything but deterministic. Outside Indonesia, all major OECD economies are yet to find a durable solution to their respective fiscal imbalances at a time when old-age dependency is rising more steeply. While the new chairwoman of the US Federal Reserve, Janet Yellen, has repeatedly stated her accommodative monetary policy stance in the tradition of “salt-water economics”, the impacts of tapering on global capital flows remain a question mark.

Japan’s growth may etiolate, if the planned sales tax increase comes into force. Closer to home, instability is looming large over Thailand and Cambodia. In fact, all of East Asia has turned in recent years into an arena of territorial disputes, which in the past were defused in the interest of economic development.

Back in Indonesia, the government budget in rupiah terms is increasingly strained with inflated debt services, the fuel subsidy and some parts of procurement. Assuming that no major initiative is launched in the early part of 2014 to control expenses, fiscal imbalance is likely to be a source of major concern to economic stability. Unfortunately, the incentive to act boldly seems to have weakened for the current coalition of government.

The legislative election in April and presidential election in July constitute another potential source of instability. How they are going to affect the economy is hard to guess. On the one hand, election spending by political parties and the government is said to have a slightly expansionary impact, though the magnitude is dampened by the revenue that is needed to finance the spending.

More importantly, policy debates between now and the early days of the next government in the last quarter of 2014 may drive domestic and foreign investors to adopt a wait-and-see attitude. The big themes of the debates include nationalism, or even “nativism” or “ethnicity-inclined provincialism”, self-reliance or a permissive attitude toward protectionist measures, including rent-laden measures, state-centered industrial policy and egalitarianism or populism.

In spite of the ASEAN Economic Community 2015, negotiation on the Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership (RCEP), which was championed by Indonesia, the success of Indonesia in hosting the APEC Summit and the WTO Ministerial Conference in Bali in 2013, economic openness is likely to come under fire in the run-up to the elections.

The ban on unprocessed mineral exports, for instance, receives support from most, if not all, political parties, though Indonesia’s small share in the global export market does not promise effective enforcement, with the exception of tin and nickel, of which Indonesia is the world’s largest exporter, with a market share in 2012 of 29 percent and 25 percent, respectively.

The call for stronger protection of the domestic market against imports and foreign ownership is also likely to rank prominently in the economic policy debates before and after the legislative election. The same applies to progressive increases in minimum wage irrespective of the eroding impacts on competitiveness and populist government programs ignorant of the threat to fiscal sustainability.

Under the crescendo of election noise, which sometimes can harm wise policy-making, the words of prudence will have to be voiced loudly and tirelessly and fulfilled by the few stewards of stability within the government in collaboration with like-minded stakeholders in the media, the business community and academia.

The finance minister, the governor of Bank Indonesia and the Financial Services Authority (OJK) should find ways to act cooperatively as a stability board, aiming at insulating the government budget, monetary policy and banking policy against opportunistic decisions by other parts of the government, which in an immature democracy incline to tell people what is pleasing to the ears while sweeping substantive issues under the carpet, such as the phasing out of subsidies on current consumption, underinvestment in infrastructure, inefficiency of government spending, the enormity of vulnerable employment, high-cost logistics and a virtual lack of corporate research and development, which in a mature democracy constitute the core issues of contention.
