Urban Chat: Let’s convict
predators – and protect
ourselves, too

Lynda Ibrahim, Jakarta | Lifestyle | Sat, April 19 2014, 12:13 PM
Lifestyle News
  • Swiss made with French touch
  • Batik with crystal touch
  • Denim code for hairstyle

I was basking in post-holiday bliss when I stumbled onto the news of the alleged sexually molested kindergartner.

After choking on my morning coffee I felt the urge to drown the perpetrators in piping hot coffee — or feed them alive to hungry lions.

And I’m not even a parent. Yet.

Parents have been in uproar since the boy’s family went to the police. And since the tragedy took place at a popular international school, many expats who tend to be isolated from domestic affairs suddenly found themselves engulfed in the issue.

Many of my neighbors have kids at that school, and their grim faces these days say it all.

Expats must juggle different educational standards at home and whatever country they may live in, so their choices are much fewer than we locals have.

Repatriating children is also not easy, for it divides the family and bleeds the budget. I work with expats in my consulting job and I know their children’s education and well-being in a foreign land, so removed from their organic safety net, far exceeds any other concern.

The protests and rants, some of which are oddly are aimed at tuition teachers’ salaries by people who don’t even enroll their kids there, sounds like schadenfreude.

More sober minds are petitioning the House of Representatives to change the law to punish child predators with sentences greater than the current 5 to 15 years’ imprisonment.

I hope that in whatever time they have left that our often disconnected legislatives can amend this law — one that actually serves their constituents.

The Education and Culture Ministry also swiftly responded to the public outcry. But just as swiftly, I stopped singing their praises after I carefully read the ministry’s post-inspection four-point statement.

The first two points addressed how the school lacked a permit for early education.

The school has been operating since the late 1970s, and has been lagging in complying with the latest law, which went into effect in January 2003.

Legal administration is squarely within the ministry’s authority, so where the heck has the ministry has been all these years?

If this tragedy hadn’t happened, would the ministry have even realized this administrative negligence? If the ministry can’t even keep track of administration, how are we supposed to expect it to produce an education blueprint — let alone integrating current issues like child pornography and sexual awareness?

While we wait for the authorities to get their act together, what have we done to protect our youngsters? I won’t pretend to know firsthand about parenting, yet allow me to humbly share two cents from my upbringing and general observations.

Early on my parents taught me that not only I’m the ultimate owner of my body, there were boundaries where others could see or touch me.

I was perhaps four when mom started mandating a towel upon leaving bathroom, and the lessons gradually expanded.

Parents can find similar concept in the Underwear Rule, a practical and child-friendly approach to teach physical boundaries (underwearrule.org).

If you set these boundaries, it’s easier to stop kids sending compromising selfies to whoever they meet online once they play with their first gadget (nowadays often as early as the age of three).

Openness is important. If I ever felt “wrong” or “funny” about my body, included in interaction with others, I had to say “No” to the person and then told my parents so they could help.

Kids often worry that telling parents things will get them into trouble, but they may sink into more trouble if they can’t seek help from parents.

A buddy system also helps. As an only child I developed independent streaks early, which might equally get constructive and destructive for curious yet innocent mind.

My parents made me have at least one friend when walking to and from school, during recess and especially on field trips. Simply put, two kids scream for help louder than one.

When all else fails, there is self-defense. “Any father will turn into a raging lion to defend his daughters, but you need to learn how to defend yourself if, God forbid, I can’t get there fast enough.” Dad, circa 1984.

The first technique they taught was to scream bloody murder and run, before later putting me into karate school. Last year I saw a garbage man on sidewalk teaching his little daughter to throw simple punches and kicks — a poor yet smart father fully aware of their daily environment. Are you?

Finally, there is a place for restraint. This is for proud parents, braggart uncles or aunties.

Not all pedophiles act their urges to become sexual molesters, but why tempt them?

Many people post endless pictures of their kids online, including while in nude (“See how cute my son taking a bath!”), complete with name and age.

Some are dimwitted enough to check into Foursquare whenever dropping off or picking up kids from school.

Don’t get me started on parents who open social media accounts for a newborn before its umbilical cord is even severed.

There’s a line between proudly parading your offspring and turning them into potential prey. Learn that line today.

God help me when I’ve got my own kids one day.
